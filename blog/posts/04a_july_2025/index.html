Retrospective on route, strategy, etc

Posted on Jul 5, 2025

There are a fair number of different ways you can do these trans-Am rides: self-supported solo, self-supported with a group, van-supported, and so on. Having been through it with the ACA I’m very happy with my decision to go van-supported and with the group, but I am a bit curious about what it would be like to do it completely solo instead.

The support van’s advantages pretty much speak for themselves – it is great from a gear perspective (van carries your stuff! yay!) and it is very helpful to have wheels in case of bike gear failures and such.

Group travel also has obvious advantages and drawbacks. It is good to have other folks to keep you company and to share the chores (cooking and such), but of course depending on the dynamics people can get on each other’s nerves, etc (it just depends).

On the other hand you lose pretty much all of your spontaneity and flexibility with a van-supported group on a schedule. Going on your own means that you can make things up as you go along, and take rest days when you want them as opposed to having them planned out.

Direction-wise I am convinced that westbound is infinitely superior to eastbound, no question in my mind. The one argument you hear (or at least that I heard when I was looking into it) for eastbound is that the prevailing winds are out of the west, so you get more tailwinds overall. On our tour, however I really didn’t find this to be the case at all – wind direction was more like a 50-50 proposition, and in fact there were bunches of rides where wind was not a serious factor in any case.

Then there is the sun: when you ride westbound you get the sun behind you in the mornings, as opposed to having to squint into it going east. This was a huge advantage.

Finally there is the matter of the Rocky mountains – going westbound means that instead of tackling the high country right away you get plenty of time to work up your endurance, so by the time you finally hit Colorado you are a way stronger biker than when you started your tour. Mountains also factor in when it comes to start date: beginning the tour on the west coast means a later start date (so as to avoid snow and such in the mountains), which in turn means a much hotter slog in the eastern part of the USA when you get there.
